Sweet Dream, Rowan

“Can I have… that one?”

Rowan pointed at the pink popsicle as Noah stood with his orange one, waiting by the counter.

Ding!

The bell clinked as the door opened. Both Rowan and Noah stepped outside the convenience store. Today was another hot day after school, and the temperature was as hot as the sun. Both students made their way onto the bench and sat down.  Rowan unwrapped his popsicle as Noah looked at him in worry.

“How's high school? Better than middle school?” Rowan questioned, taking a bite.

“Yeah, school’s fine,” Noah answered half-heartedly.

“What's wrong?” Rowan asked without looking away.

“Are you sure you’re okay? Did nothing happen in the mountains when you were missing?”

It had already been a few weeks since Rowan had disappeared, but Noah was still worried. The blond-haired boy smiled as if he was consoling a puppy.

“Yes, I’m fine. Now eat your popsicle before it starts melting.”

Not realizing Rowan had ignored his second question, Noah hesitantly opened his popsicle and took a bite.

“It’s already been a few months, but you haven’t attended school. How come?”

“Aww, were you missing me? I can imagine poor Noah crying, ‘Rowan won’t come to school!’” Rowan laughed, his glasses sliding down his nose.

“Hey, cut it out!” Noah smiled. “Still…”

“I told you, I’m fine.”

Noah took another bite, his gaze on Rowan.

“It’s just that… you’re so different from before.”

Rowan stopped as a bead of sweat trickled down his forehead. He averted his gaze to his popsicle and chuckled nervously.

“You’re just imagining things. I’m still your best friend!”

He looked back and elbowed Noah.

Noah grinned.

Splat!

His popsicle fell to the ground, some parts still on the stick.

“Yeah. You’re probably right.”

* * *

“I’m back, mum.”

Noah said, taking off his shoes at the entrance of his home. His mother appeared from the hallway in her yellow apron, a ladle in hand.

“Hey, hun. How was school? You’re late.”

“I was just hanging out with Rowan after school.”

“Uhm, alright hun.” His mother had an awkward smile, her expression one of worry.

Noah climbed up the stairs and into his room. It was a simple bedroom with a plain bed and desk. Noah wasn’t too keen on decorating. He only owned a framed picture of him and Rowan in middle school on his desk. Noah smiled, pulling out his homework while looking at that picture.

 It had already been four hours after he got home when Noah realized it had gotten late. Suddenly, a noise rang in his head. It was akin to the noise of a bell, he realized. It rang repeatedly, the sound shrieking in his mind. Along with the bell, another noise started. It was a sentence, Noah realized.

“WHAT SHOULD YOU DO IF YOU SEE A CLASSMATE GETTING BULLIED?”

Noah covered his ears and ground his teeth, wishing this would all be over.

“NOTHING!”

 Finally, when Noah collapsed on his bed, it stopped. He groaned in annoyance and sat up. Noah reached for his bedside table and pulled out a pill bottle. It read Aripiprazole. Noah shook out a few and swallowed it, laying back on his bed. He shut his eyes, his breath light. Noah thought about Rowan, and how he reacted after what Noah had said. Soon, his conscience drifted off to sleep.

“Hey, Pass the ball!”

Middle school students yelled at Rowan as he ran with the soccer ball, narrowly missing the other team members. Once near, he took one powerful kick and scored a goal. His team members rushed him with praise. Sitting on the sidelines, Noah smiled as everyone beside him cheered.

“Rowan is so cool!”

“He is so cute as well.”

“You think he’s going to become a pro soccer player?”

They all agreed that Rowan would be a fantastic athlete in the future, and at one point Noah thought it too. However, this sinking feeling inside his stomach wouldn’t stop. Despite the bright sky, clear weather, and happy expressions everyone had, Noah couldn’t help but have a strange feeling in his gut, like this was going wrong. Noah flinched at the sound of the bell. Wide-eyed, he looked up at the bell tower, the ringing getting louder and louder.

“I’m sorry…”

He muttered uncontrollably. His eyebrows lowered, his mouth trying to utter a sentence. Noah’s body shook, and he forced his eyes off the tower to the ground. Hunched over he hugged his stomach, sweat dripping from his forehead.

“I’m…”

“...Sorry.”

That last word came out as a small whisper. Noah was lying on his side in bed, his arms tightly wrapped around his body. He was shivering.

Noah sat up and put his palm on his forehead.

That dream again.

He thought, sighing as he got up to get ready for school.

* * *

In the peaceful campus of Redwood High School, everything in the classrooms is supervised and taken care of by the class representatives. However, Kaiya recently noticed something strange. A boy in her classroom has been acting differently than normal. Compared to the start of the year, Noah Gray has been looking a lot more– rough, –as she calls it. The dark circles under his eyes have worsened, his black hair has gotten longer, and he hasn’t been eating lunch. Not at school, at least. Having a strong sense of responsibility, she asked some classmates if they had any information on Noah. The only thing she heard? An incident in middle school that happened right before summer.

“It was crazy.” One friend explained.

“I heard it was really bad.” Another expressed.

“No one saw the car coming.”

But that was all she could find. No one had explained what happened or knew what happened. Although, one name kept popping up– Rowan Ellis. He was described as a boy with blonde hair and glasses. Rowan peaked in middle school, but she heard he had disappeared a few weeks ago. Other than that, she was sure he didn’t attend Redwood.

After school, Kaiya trudged through the road of her small town. A corner store was just a few blocks from the school, and a popsicle sounded good in the warm weather.

Just as she rounded the block, she came upon a strange sight. Kaiya encountered Noah, except he was smiling.

She inched closer, determined to see what he was so happy about. However, nothing was there. For a moment, she stood there confused, wondering what to make of this sight. Noah was talking and smiling on an empty bench. Was this guy insane? A shiver ran down Kaiya’s spine, and she inched backward. A gut feeling warned her about Noah, especially not to be alone with him. Whatever that was, instinctively, she stepped back. With her heart hammering in her chest, Kaiya turned and ran, her pace speeding up as she got farther away. Eventually, in a full sprint, Kaiya could hear the blood rushing to her head. She only stopped when the convenience store was far away. She didn’t get it. She didn’t know why she was so scared. It was just something about that sight, about how you know someone is seeing something completely different than you. Looking at Noah’s face made her realize that he was far beyond anyone’s reach.

School meant nothing to Noah. He had average grades and didn’t join any clubs. He had a few friends, but he was sure if he just stopped coming to school one day, they wouldn’t notice. But Noah didn’t care, because he still had his best friend Rowan. After school every day they would talk to each other at the convenience store, and it always seemed like Noah's troubles would all fade away.

Noah turned the page of his book. Sitting in his seat in the Homeroom, he thought about what to eat later with Rowan. He was instead jarred out of his thoughts, confused and wondering why Kaiya, his class representative, was standing before him.

“Noah, can I… talk to you?”

Her voice shivered.

Noah followed her out of the classroom and into the barren hallways. No one else was there except him and Kaiya, making Noah uneasy.

Kaiya kept twiddling her fingers and shifting her weight from one leg to the other. Her eyes were on the ground and she never made eye contact with him.

“Listen, Noah, what do you do after school every day?”

Stunned by this question, he hesitated before answering,

“I hang out with my friend. Why?”

“Uhm…who is this friend?”

“Rowan.”

Shocked, Kaiya stepped back like she had seen a ghost. Her eyes were wide, and her tone was grim.

“But… I heard Rowan was dead.”

Noah froze, a shudder going through his body.

“No… NO!”

He ran up and grabbed Kaiya’s shoulders.

“I know, I know what you’re thinking. It’s not like that! He’s not–”

“He disappeared two weeks ago!” Kaiya yelled with her eyes closed. “He… disappeared two weeks ago…” She repeated.

Noah let go, and she collapsed on the ground, shuddering.

“He’s real,” Noah muttered.

“Rowan is… there.”

Noah’s yelling had alerted his classmates, and now people were beginning to crowd them. Unsure what to do, Noah’s only goal was to get out of there.

Sprinting out of the school and off campus, Noah ran to the convenience store where Rowan would be. It was too early, but Noah was determined to wait for him. He had to see him again.

Maybe Kaiya was right…

Sitting on the bench, Noah shook his head and clenched his fist.

No! I saw him yesterday! He was right in front of me! They're all wrong!

He hunched over, covering his face with his hands.

He was right there.

Hours went, way past the time they were supposed to meet. For some odd reason, Rowan had never let Noah stay with him after dark, but until nightfall, Noah stayed there, sitting on the bench, solely waiting for Rowan.

Ding! The owner shuffled out of the store, getting ready to close up for the night. Glancing over to the bench, she jumped in surprise at the sight of Noah, sitting on the bench lifeless.

Has he been here since the morning?

The owner inched closer and tapped her finger on his shoulder. He flinched and looked around before facing her.

“Oh… Hey Ms. Millian. Is Rowan here?”

“...I think it's time for you to go home.”

She replied.

“What? Why? What’s wrong?”

“Just go, kid.”

“Wait! I still need to–”

“GO!”

Noah flinched at her yelling and started to get up to leave.

Arriving at his house, he started taking his shoes off at the entrance when the sound of talking started. Noah peeked into the living room to see his parents, not aware he was home.

“It seems like nothing is working…”

His mother sobbed, her hands covering her face. Noah’s father had a grim expression on his face, his hand patting her back.

“I’m sure everything is alright.”

His father chided. Sensing danger, Noah left and quietly crept up the stairs and into his room. Even though he shut and locked the door, he could still hear his mothers crying.

“Nothing is ‘alright’! Don’t you know what he said?” His mother cried. “Noah said… he met Rowan…”

His father sighed, exasperated.

“Ever since he went to the mountains with Rowan… nothing has been the same.”

“You don’t think…” His mother let out a sob.

“I’m just saying, honey. Perhaps…”

Noah sat down and leaned against the door, his eyes locked onto the floor.

“Don’t you know how embarrassed I am to have a hallucinating son?”

Noah shut his eyes tight.

Rowan is not a hallucination. He’s real… am I the problem? Why do I have to be so useless? Why am I like this?

Noah had never asked to be this way. He had never asked to be himself, so why does it hurt so much?

“Noah? Your home pretty late…are you hungry? Should I cook something up?”

His mother called.

“No. I’m not hungry.”

Noah’s voice shook.

Noah didn’t go back to school. He simply couldn’t. He couldn’t handle all the stares, all the questions. Every single day. Every single day he had to go to school and sit there, sit there feeling empty, sit there and feel guilty for just existing. He hated every single second, every single minute of being alive. Noah didn’t want to go to sleep, he didn’t want to wake up either. He felt alone because Noah knew they were no other person who felt this way. And he knew he shouldn’t be feeling this way. He has loving parents, a home, and a best friend. But there was just something about waking up in the morning and staring outside the window, wishing he could be someone different. A person who’s not crazed up in the head. Maybe then he wouldn’t be such a burden to his parents.  It’s enough that he causes them so much stress. He didn’t need to cause them any more trouble. But Noah knew if he ever committed, his mother would cry. And he didn’t know how to handle that.

 At 3:10, Noah crept out to wait for Rowan. He needed something to take his mind off, to reassure himself that he was right.

Rounding the corner, Noah’s eyes were locked on the bench by the store. To his relief. There he was, a blonde boy with glasses was sitting on the bench.

Noah’s eyes lit up as his worries faded away. He sprinted to Rowan, clasping him in a tight hug.

“Noah? What’s wrong?”

Noah sighed in relief. Rowan was real. He felt real. Noah knew it was all lies. Why would Rowan ever lie to him?

After calming down, Noah told Rowan the whole story, how Kaiya had lied about Rowan’s death and how his parents didn’t believe him.

Noah expected Rowan’s reaction to be one of anger or surprise. But what he didn’t expect was tears. Rowan was crying.

“Rowan? What’s wrong?”

Panic overtook Noah, and his face dropped.

“Tell me what’s wrong. I’ll fix it, so please don’t cry…”

It’s just like that time.

Noah thought.

 His mind was going blank, the only thing he could think of was that scene, that scene of middle-school Noah standing outside Rowan’s room. Noah didn’t see him that day. The only thing he heard was crying. Rowan was crying because of Noah.

“It’s just that…” Rowan sobbed. “I just feel so helpless. I don’t care if people make stuff up… but for you to believe them?”

“No!”

Noah blurted.

“Of course, I don’t believe them!” He laughed. “Y-you’re right here! Your real, Rowan.”

“Then… What should we do?”

“We should prove to them that you’re not dead!” Noah grinned.

“Just so you don’t seem crazy?”

Noah’s face dropped.

“W-what?”

Rowan smiled, wiping the tears away.

“Nothing. I know how to do that. Hey, Noah, come and meet me in the mountains tomorrow night. Okay? That’s how we’ll prove that I’m real.”

“Are you sure?”

Rowan patted Noah’s head.

“Whenever have I been wrong?”

* * *

His mother didn’t question it when he said he didn’t want to go to school.

That was the good part of being insane, I guess.

Noah would think. Sitting in his room and nervously tapping his finger on his leg Noah found it hard to sit still. It was finally tomorrow, the day when Noah was going to prove to everyone that he wasn’t crazy, that Rowan was alive. He didn’t know how he was going to prove it, but Noah trusted Rowan. Surely he didn’t lie to him. Noah was tired of ignoring it, ignoring all the things people were saying about his best friend. He decided to finally put an end to this.

“Don’t meet me in the daytime... Just go to the mountains straight away.”

“Don’t meet… at day… mountains straight away…”

Noah repeated Rowan’s words in his mind while waiting for the sun to go down. Finally, it was 7 PM, time for Noah to leave.

He burst open his door and went downstairs. Putting on his shoes, his mother appeared from the hallway.

“Noah? What are you going this late at night?”

“Just some things with Rowan. You’ll see!”

“Wait, Noah–”

But before his mother could do anything, Noah was already out the door. He sprinted around the corner and past the convenience store, past the houses, and onto the path up the mountain. About to make his way up, Noah froze. Did Rowan tell him to wait or go up? Noah shook his head.

He said to meet him in the mountains. He must have meant going up.

Trudging up the mountain while the day was ending left Noah alone with his thoughts. He felt guilty, guilty about Rowan.

Maybe today things would go back to normal.

Noah thought, smiling.

Finally, he made it to a clearing. It was a large circular area, the edge hidden by trees.

“Noah.”

Noah whirled around to find no one.

Maybe it was the wind.

“Noah!”

He flinched, looking in all directions. There a few feet ahead of him he found Rowan.

“Rowan!”

Just as Noah took a step, Rowan darted out of the clearing and into the trees.

“Wait!”

Noah ran after him, the shadows of the trees dancing and the wind laughing as the branches nicked his arms and legs.

“Rowan! Wait!”

He was getting too far, and soon he was out of sight. Noah stopped and huffed, wiping away his sweat. He looked around, but Rowan was nowhere to be seen. Just as he was about to call again, a hand jumped out of the bushes and grabbed Noah’s shirt, pulling him out and into a clearing. It was Rowan’s.

“Rowan! What are you–”

“Noah.”

Rowan spoke. Noah quieted.

“Why do you pretend?”

Noah’s mouth curled into an awkward smile.

“W-what?”

Noah asked.

“Why do you pretend to care? You didn’t pretend back then.”

There it was again, that guilty feeling.

“You’re right. I’m sorry. I’m sorry. It was my fault.”

Noah mumbled.

“What was your fault?”

“Everything. I hurt you, Rowan. It's… all my fault…”

Noah’s smile dropped, and he stared at the dirt floor.

“I’m the reason you can’t play… I ignored it when they tore your stuff. I ignored it when those scars appeared…” He clenched his fists. “But today, I’m going to make it up to you, so just don’t…”

Noah looked up.

“Don’t!”

The space in front of Noah wasn’t there. He was nowhere to be seen.

“Hate…me?”

The wind whistled in his ear, the trees dancing in the breeze. The moon shone down as Noah slowly turned, his eyes locked on the old rotting tree behind him. Rowan had never gone down from the mountains, Noah realized. Because the decaying body in front of him with broken glasses and blonde hair was Noah’s fault. It was all because of him.

*It was ironic, really.*

The thought implanted itself (unintentionally and uninvitingly) in Roxanne Adaline’s head as she let out a sniffle matching the pairs of tears collected at the edge of her jaw sitting on the dark brown and comfortingly soft living room couch. The fifteen-year-old had never really felt grief before, a fortunate privilege which she will always consider herself lucky to have. But not experiencing such a strong emotion just made it hit so, so much harder. It also didn’t help at all taking in that the person she lost was one of - if not the most - influential person in Roseanne’s life apart from her parents: her beloved Nonna.

She was suddenly disrupted out of her melancholy musings as she felt her father’s tight embrace release itself and her mom following the action. You see, Roseanne wasn’t just sitting on her couch, she was squished in-between the protective and almost painful grip of the two mourning parents, her mom hugging her hunched back as Rose sat practically on her dad’s legs while she childishly, but reasonably clutched his middle. They probably looked like a family of clingy monkeys in retrospect but none of them cared as their bond grew with shared sadness.

“Hey there, Rosie Posie, ” her father breathed out, obviously not surprised yet still concerned by her newly split tears. She had been crying, from silent drops to heart-wrenching sobs, for what seemed like at least a few hours but ran out of water to let out about a half-hour ago, leaving the living room full of easily punctured quietness. “You okay, tesoro?” he said, using his hands to wipe off any leftover tears from his face before doing the same for her. She couldn’t help but lean into the touch.

Though Daniel Luca Adaline (a pretty awesome name, isn’t it? Rose always thought it was) didn’t live in his mother home in Italy anymore, the man remained a dedicated Italian, meaning he always found many excuses to make pizza, pasta, and spaghetti, gush about random art and philosophy facts to act smart - which never failed to annoy Mamma-, and always chose gelato over ice cream any day. His mom also made sure that her son learned and memorized to the bone as much of his mother tongue as possible before he moved to the US and became surrounded by English speakers. Daniel, just like his mother did for him, brought out the Italian nicknames for his child whenever in distress: when Rose was a baby and started teething which resulted in a painful day of screaming, when she fell down and skidded her knees in first grade, when she burnt her finger at 10 trying to make a Father’s Day breakfast, and even when she grew up and was faced with the harsh reality of high school last year and needed consultation from him through soft murmurs.

Whatever good intention Daniel had was replaced with even more sorrow as Rose was knocked off her feet with the realization that the same parental nicknames her dad was calling her were identical to the ones his mother used to say - not just his mother, but her Nonna. She couldn't handle the sharp and somewhat physical pain that was felt in her chest as Rose’s heart broke for her father, the man who had raised her through hardships and had now lost his mom long after losing his other parent decades before. “I’m -”, her tattered and broken voice tore through the awaiting silence, “Ahem, I’m just - just really going to miss Nonna, Papà”.

“Well, it’s alright to feel like that, ragazzino. We’re all going to miss her a hecking lot, me, you, your mamma, even those two crazy best friends of yours,” her dad said gently but firmly, making sure his daughter knew that crying was a normal and expected way to deal with loss and understand that her family and friends would always be there for her. Especially, when experiencing loss.

He grinned when Rose couldn’t help the snort and giggle that found its way up her throat at the mention of her friends and opened her mouth to respond with a quip to playfully defend her friends, but then furrowed her brows as she asked herself an important question.

*Should I still go to the ‘end-of-the-year’ dance today with Aurora and Flynn? I don’t really feel in the mood to party right now; But we’ve been planning our outfits forever and knowing them, my heart-warmingly (but sometimes stupidly) loyal best friends probably won’t attend if I don’t…*

        “Sweetie? Whatcha’ thinkin’ about in that brain of yours?” Rose’s thoughts were again interrupted but this time by her mom - well mom of 3 years after Aubrey Clara Sinter married her dad after many romantic run-ins at Daniel’s favorite coffee shop. And unlike most of the “step-mom scenarios” shown on Netflix, Aubrey was great to hang out within the start of their relationship and it was only two years ago since Rose started calling her ‘Mom.”

        “Should I still go to the dance in a few hours, Mamma? I know I’m still going to feel sad over Nonna even in a few hours but I still… I still kind of want to go and at least try to make something good of today. I mean, it’s the school end celebration and I think that Nonna -” she has to cleanse her throat when it closes up at the verbal mention of her passed grandmother. “- she would want me to attend the dance today”.

        Without lifting her head even an inch, Rose could tell that her parents gave each other a look over her top layer of messy and uncombed hair that still hid an even messier hidden blanket of uncombed and frizzy tangles. She could practically hear the concerned and contemplating conversation taking place back and forth between the two adults but kept quiet, desperately needing a wise answer. Rose heard and felt her dad take a deep breath and lightly extracted her from his arms before responding in what could only be described as a careful manner, “To be honest miele, it’s up to you to make that call, but don’t think that you have to go to the dance in any way, shape, or form, okay? If you do, then we’ll quickly call over your chaotic friends and have them take you to dance for a few hours before it gets too dark.”

        Through all the heavy tones felt in the room, Rose grinned and huffed a laugh while jokingly smacking her dad’s chest and retaliated, “Stop making fun of Aurora and Flynn, Papà!”. That brought a genuine smile onto everyone on that couch’s faces.

“Okay, okay, but there’s also another option ragazzino so listen up,” and she hummed to display her allowance, “we can also just order some Chinese takeout and Dominos pizza, save that amazing dress for your birthday in a few months, and maybe even call over your ‘besties’ to join us in some Buzzfeed Unsolved binge-watching!” Rose thought, as much as she loved the idea of the latter, she still wanted to have that cliché but iconic “prom experience.”

So she mentally nodded and gathered up her courage to look up at her dad and finalize her decision, “I wanna go, papà; And will you help me get ready, mamma?”

Her dad smiled with a sense of understanding and hints of pride as Aubrey replied with strong certainty, “Definitely, baby, I’ll go get your outfit set up”. Her mom got up from the couch with one last kiss to Rose’s head as her dad cupped his child’s cheek’s to make sure she was listening to his next words.

“Just always remember bambina, crying doesn’t indicate that you’re weak because, since birth, it’s always been a sign that you’re alive.”

Just the consideration of the first two emotions she saw in them made her tear up and sniffle which was more than enough for Aurora and Flynn to fling her into a bone-crushing hug that rivaled only one of her parents.

Rose knew this and it made her even sadder, if her non-family support system was in the same grieving boat as her, how could they help?
