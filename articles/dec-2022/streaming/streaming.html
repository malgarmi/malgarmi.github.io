Is Streaming Killing the Music Industry?

By: Isaiah Encinas

        Streaming. Ever since its inception it has generated billions of dollars in revenue, bringing in 12.4 billion dollars in 2021 alone. However, its reputation in the music industry is a very broad subject, with some of the biggest artists in the world having expressed their views and opinions on it. Like Taylor Swift, who in 2014 removed her catalog from Spotify, eventually returning in 2017 and having a love-hate relationship with the company ever since. So why is it disliked by musicians? Well, you have to know its history first.

Origin

        The first streaming service was founded in 1989 under the name of Xing Technologies, which created Streamworks, a JPEG streaming product. Soon after that, Starworks was established by Starlight Networks and allowed people to access MPEG-1 full-motion videos via corporate Ethernet networks. However, it wasn’t until 1999 that the music industry broke out in the form of Napster.

        Using a peer-to-peer file sharing network, Napster allowed people to upload and download MP3 files. This format of code contains compressed sound that could be replayed to the liking of its owner. It was one of the first forms of free “music streaming”, and at the time it was running garnered millions of users. But, in 2001, Napster would face lawsuits regarding copyright infringement and fair use laws due to the sharing of music without the consent of its creators. Years later, Napster would be bought out by another company called Rhapsody, which is known as the first paid streaming service. Rhapsody had a catalog composed of licensed music from labels like Universal Records and allowed people to listen to it if they paid a monthly fee.

        In the years following Rhapsody, more streaming services have launched, most notably ones like Apple Music, Pandora, and Spotify, all of which have established their presence in the industry. And as time grew, so did their impact on music.

Effects

        Since its creation, music streaming has only grown. From 2015 to 2021, the amount of paid subscribers for these services went from 68 million to 523.9 million. As this increased, so did their profit, as according to the Headphones Addict, the revenue generated went from 100 million in 2005 to 16.9 billion in 2021, around a 37.5% increase yearly.

        Music streaming has impacted artists as well. Statista recorded physical CD shipments from 1999, and they have steadily decreased since then, going from 938.99 million copies to only 46.6 million copies in 2021. This is also evident in artists' first-week sales. Take Kanye West for an example, whose 2007 album Graduation sold 957,000 copies (both physical and streamed) in the first week, compared to his most recent album Donda, which only sold 327,270 copies in its first week. This is because people stream songs rather than buy physical copies. The RIAA (Recording Industry Association of America) stated that 1,500 streams or listens online would equate to 1 physical sale to help streaming gain more traction in the industry as a viable way to release music.

        Services like Spotify and Soundcloud also make it easier for artists to get their names out to listeners and get signed by big labels. Before streaming, and really before the internet, artists achieved fame strictly off of word of mouth, which they achieved from live performances. Then the internet became more developed, which artists took advantage of. As the Michigan Daily says, Soulja Boy was one of the first musicians to embrace the internet, who posted his music on

the internet and created websites that advertised his songs. And now, we see people post on sites like Soundcloud, which allows them to reach audiences internationally.

        Streaming as a whole has brought a whole new way for artists and listeners alike to enjoy and produce music. However, under all the good hides a secret.

Why The Hate?

In recent years, music services have had a reputation for having low compensation for their artists, and they pay this compensation in one main way: Royalty Distribution. Royalty distribution is the method of paying all the credits a certain percentage of the revenue from the song. According to SoundExchange, the money is generally split between the featured artists (45%), non-featured artists (5%), and the rights owners (50%).

While these splits may seem fair at first glance, it’s the payouts that have been critiqued. The USA Today reports that an artist named Mackenzie Miller had 14,000 streams on her single in its first week, yet only earned $5 from it.This is due to the extremely low pay for each stream,

which a large percentage of goes to the rights owners, or the people artists depend on to represent them. The rights owners are also the people who pay the artists, so before artists are paid, their money has gone through a multitude of groups first.

Another reason for the distaste towards music streaming services is the fact that it has become oversaturated. Over 11 million artists are on Spotify alone, yet over half of them have fewer than 50 monthly listeners. There are simply too many people creating music all at once for everyone to have their deserved attention, especially independent artists that make up the vast majority of Spotify’s catalog.

Due to these reasons as well as a plethora of others, many artists both independent and signed have publicly sowed their sentiment towards the music streaming industry, both by statements in the press and their actions. However, does this mean streaming is killing the music industry?

The Future

        Streaming as we know it is the way of moving forward in music. As music like any other art grows, so do its artists, and to do that streaming their music is simply the best way. So while streaming may be a cause for concern, it has become irreplaceable for both the industry and its artists.
