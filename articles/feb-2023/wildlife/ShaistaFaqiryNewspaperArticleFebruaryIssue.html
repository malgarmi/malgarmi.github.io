      Lamarckdromia beagle

 

        A strange crab was discovered washed up on a beach near Denmark, Western Australia, in the spring of 2022, in the month of April. The crab was found by a family, who then took it to the Western Australian Museum to learn more about this unusual crab. The species was researched by scientists and was not classified yet.

        The Indian Ocean and the Western Australian coastlines are home to the Lamarckdromia beagle. The Lamarckdromia beagle lives in shallow waters few meters deep. The crab hides at the bottom of rocky shorelines for camouflage and protection.

        The Lamarckdromia beagle is a crab that belongs to the Dromiidae family of sponge crabs. A Dromiidae crab can range in size from being small to medium size. The Lamarckdromia beagle can have a transportable shelter by living beneath its coat of sponge. The crab also belongs to the family of crustaceans, which are marine organisms that live in shallow seas. Barnacles, lobsters, shrimp, and other sea creatures are examples of crustaceans.  The Lamarckdromia beagle can gather and cut marine sponges and can wear them on its shell.  The crab can also have ascidians, known as sea squirters on their shells. The ascidians and marine sponges the crab uses can offer safety. The sponge the crab gathers has chemicals that can cause their predators to flee. The crab's sponge also serves as a type of camouflage, which can protect the species from predators. Tlalocohyla Celeste   

On a rewilded nature reserve in Costa Rica, the Tlalocohyla Celeste was found. The Tapir Valley Nature Reserve is the name of the reserve. The nature reserve protects creatures like peccaries, jaguars, and baird’s tapirs. Donald Valera Soto, the reserve’s co-owner, was once working in the wetlands of the nature reserve, until he heard an unfamiliar frog call. Donald talked with a website called, news.mongabay.com and said, “I kept hearing this different sound in the wetland but was unable to find it.” He had never heard that sound before, and Donald had searched for the frog for six months, until he discovered it. Donald discovered the species and it was two centimeters long. To identify the new species, Donald had help from herpetologists, researchers who study amphibians and reptiles all around the world. Some people thought it was a canal zone tree frog, another amphibian in Costa Rica, but the frog calls of the two species were different. Donald kept looking for the frog, but this time he had a team with him. His team were able to discover a male and female frog in an amplexus. An amplexus is a type of mating behavior for some amphibians. They were able to recognize the female's eggs in the amplexus. Then they had identified the new species after studying the adults and their tadpoles. The new species was named "Celeste", after a river in Costa Rica, called Rio Celeste.

The Tapir Valley Nature Reserve has the Tlalcohyla Celeste’s natural habitat, living in a wetland system with herbaceous plants and both aquatic and terrestrial ecosystems. The Tlalcohyla can be found in Costa Rica and in Central America, between Mexico.

The Tlalcohyla Celeste is a part of the Hylidae family of Tlalocohylas. The Tlalocohyla Celeste is an amphibian and is a stunning frog that is entirely covered in green. The frog’s head, back, and lower limbs are all covered with red dots. Also the skin of the Tlalocohyla Celeste is also somewhat transparent.

                                Otus bikegila

                                             

      A small island off the coast of Guinea in Africa, called Principe Island, was where the Otus bikegila (O-Tis-bike-HEE-LUH) was found. The specimen’s particular call led to its discovery. Its call resembled a call from an insect that was constantly repeated. On the southern area of the island, the Otus bikegila can be found living in the native forest. The owl was given the name “Bikegila'' in honor of Ceciliano de Bom Jesus, also known as Bikegila. Ceciliano was a parrot harvester on Principe Island, but now is a ranger, protecting the island’s natural park.

         In the southern area of Principe Island, the Otus bikegila can be found in its natural habitat living in the native forest.

        The Otus bikegila belongs to the Strigidae family and is a part of the species of scops-owls. The Otus bikegila’s size is small, but the females are often bigger than the males. The Otus bikegila can be colored in a variety of brown tones, each owl being different. Also, due to the species only on Principe Island, the Otus bikegila is potentially in serious risk to be an endangered species, which is devastating as the species can be extinct any time soon. Luckily, the forest the owl lives in, is a confined area and can not be entered.
