Introduction

Plastics have always been a problem for the environment, as it takes almost one thousand years to decompose. Over half of the plastic products produced are only used once and not recycled, the remaining laid to rest in our landfills and oceans. According to publichealthpost.org, “As of 2017, the world produced 8.3 billion metric tons of plastic”, but only around 10% of it was recycled. Around one-tenth of this total plastic waste is polystyrene, also called styrofoam. The hydrophobic and high molecular properties (or chemical and physical making of the substance) of the substance make biodegradation of styrofoam extremely difficult.

If Earth itself struggles to degrade styrofoam, then what can? Safe alternatives to break down plastic weren’t known, until now. The ‘Superworm’ was recently discovered to have the capability to digest styrofoam. Scientists from the University of Queensland in Brisbane, Australia, found that the larva of a darkling beetle; zophobas morio; can digest styrofoam. Since styrofoam has a 0.8% recycling rate, this amazing discovery can help remove as much as 30% of landfills worldwide.

Experiments and Worm Microbiomes

There is a possible way for these worms to solely survive on eating styrofoam, drawn from the conclusions of various experiments. Many tests were conducted, such as splitting a group of superworms into three other groups and feeding each of the groups a different diet. One group was fed polystyrene, another bran, and the third group had a fasting diet. This experiment lasted for three weeks. “The super worms that were fed a diet of just polystyrene not only survived, but even had marginal weight gains,” said Dr. Rinke, a scientist working on the project.

“The change in microbial gut communities from baseline differed considerably between diet groups, with polystyrene and starvation groups characterized by a loss of microbial diversity and the presence of opportunistic pathogens. Inferred microbial functions enriched in the polystyrene group included transposon movements, membrane restructuring and adaptations to oxidative stress,” the National Center for Biotechnology Information (NCBI) states.

The group that had a fasting diet started committing cannibalism 26 days into the test. The worms would eat the frailer and meager ones that did not have the potential to survive. The bran group gained the most weight and was overall the healthiest, with a 100% survival rate. On the eleventh day of testing, the styrofoam group's survival rates dropped to 98%. Five days later, the survival rates dropped again to 95%. The fasting diet group had a survival rate of 98% starting on the twenty-sixth day and stayed consistent from then on. Each group remained active throughout the experiment, with the bran group being the most liveliest, then the styrofoam, and finally, the fasting group.

The image above is a graph showing the three group’s survival rate over the course of the test. The blue line represents the bran group’s rate, the red line represents the styrofoam group’s rate, and the green line represents the fasting group’s rate.

        “Superworms are like mini recycling plants, shredding the polystyrene with their mouths and then feeding it to the bacteria in their gut. The breakdown products from this reaction can then be used by other microbes to create high-value compounds such as bioplastics,” says Dr. Rinke. The co-author of the research, PhD candidate Jiarui Sun, said they aim to grow the gut bacteria in the lab.  “We can look into how we can upscale this process to a level required for an entire recycling plant,” Ms. Sun said.

Concluding Thoughts

Plastic in general is a devastating problem, and having superworms consuming a large percentage of our styrofoam can have a decent impact. Our landfills and oceans could have reduced waste, but the superworm can’t do it on their own. Though their bacterial enzyme can benefit the planet, super worms eating polystyrene as their only food source would make them susceptible to disease or pathogenic bacteria. Although the worms themselves can not combat the amount of plastic in landfills at the moment, the bacterial enzyme is more than likely able to boost the rate of recycling greatly. As of right now, we will have to make do and try to reduce the amount of plastic and styrofoam waste for a stable future.
